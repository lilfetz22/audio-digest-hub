## TLDR

A simultaneous game-theoretic equilibrium where neither player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy.

## Body

In traditional pursuit-evasion differential games, the saddle-point equilibrium represents a state of total balance between the pursuer and the evader. Because both players act simultaneously, the mathematical solution requires solving a coupled set of differential equations representing the optimality conditions for both sides.

Historically, this has been an extremely difficult task because these systems are highly sensitive to initial conditions. Small deviations in the starting state can lead to the 'blowing up' of the numerical simulation, making it unreliable for complex or long-duration maneuvers.

## Counterarguments / Data Gaps

The saddle-point equilibrium is often considered the most 'fair' or 'realistic' representation of competitive games where neither party has an inherent hierarchical advantage. Moving to a Stackelberg equilibrium may lead to biased outcomes that do not accurately represent real-world tactical environments where players act in parallel.
