## TLDR

Performance improvements attributed to AI use are often confounded by the pre-existing motivation and skill levels of the users who choose to engage with the technology.

## Body

Selection bias occurs when individuals self-select into treatment groups (AI usage) based on characteristics that are also correlated with the outcome (performance). In the context of AI tools, highly motivated or inherently skilled individuals are more likely to seek out AI feedback. Consequently, the observed performance gains are not necessarily caused by the AI tool itself, but rather by the underlying traits of the users who choose to utilize it.

Research suggests that when these latent factors—motivation and baseline skill—are statistically controlled, the direct causal impact of AI on performance is significantly lower than initial raw data might indicate. The AI acts as a tool for those already on a high-performance trajectory, rather than a universal catalyst for improvement among all users.

## Counterarguments / Data Gaps

Critics argue that while selection bias exists, it may not entirely negate the impact of AI; some tools might provide genuine cognitive scaffolding that lowers the barrier to entry for lower-skilled users. Furthermore, if AI tools are designed to be accessible, the 'motivation' required to use them might decrease over time, potentially mitigating the selection effect.
