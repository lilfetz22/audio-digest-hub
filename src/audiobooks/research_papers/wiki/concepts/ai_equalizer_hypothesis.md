## TLDR

The belief that AI tools primarily benefit lower-skilled workers by raising their performance floor, potentially closing the gap between novice and expert performers.

## Body

The 'AI Equalizer' hypothesis posits that large language models and analytical AI agents act as a bridge for skill disparities. By automating complex cognitive tasks or providing real-time diagnostic feedback, AI theoretically allows lower-performing individuals to produce work at a level comparable to more experienced or skilled counterparts.

However, research by Riedl and Bogert suggests this may be an illusion. If the AI provides feedback that requires a high degree of foundational knowledge to interpret, it may actually widen the gap. Instead of helping those at the bottom, the tool may be disproportionately beneficial to those who already possess the domain expertise necessary to leverage AI insights effectively.

## Counterarguments / Data Gaps

The counter-narrative suggests that even if the gap remains or widens, the absolute performance of lower-skilled users still improves significantly compared to a world without AI. Thus, even if the 'relative' gap persists, the 'absolute' floor is still raised.
